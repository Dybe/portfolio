 Iterative Design

Overview and Pre-Design Thinking

In this project, I got to collaborate  with three other classmates in the iterative design process of a mobile interface. We chose to design a mobile application interface for a start-up that uses users’ preferences and biological data to generate recommendations for appropriate contraception methods. For better recommendations we encourage users to order our Birth Control Optimization Test which contains DNA and Hormone collection kits.

We decided to design a mobile application in order to reach more users and allow our users to be able to access our interface from anywhere and anytime. We wanted the entire user experience to revolve around our quiz but also added features that will supplement the quiz and give our app the potential to be an all-encompassing birth control assistant.

Sketching

As a fist step of the iterative design process, we all produced hand-drawn sketches of what we imagined the app could look like.

Lo-fi Prototype (Wireframes)

We then went over our hand drawn sketches and decided that the most important features of the app would be the preferences quiz; a symptom log where users can keep track of their response to the birth control; and a blog where they could read content on issues surrounding contraception.

We then used balsamiq to create wireframes that represented a more unified idea of how we wanted the application to look like.

Hi-fi Prototype

We then got the following feedback from our classmates:

  • Our app had a lot unnecessary text that made it appear cluttered
  • The navigation bar “look[ed] weird”. It was unclear what the “a” icon stood for.
  • The Quiz interface was confusing as questions were stylized the same as the answer options.
  • Unrelated items next to each other horizontally on the home page was not very intuitive and visually unappealing.
  • The title “home” on the homescreen was not necessary.
  • There were several cases of inconsistent typography.

Refined Hi-fi Prototype

We made changes to our first prototype to address the above issues. We also changed the theme colors to pink and black which we thought would be more visually appealing.

User Testing

We then proceeded to perform user testing on our prototype using usertesting.com.We asked testers to our app to complete the preferences quiz, read a blog post and log symptoms.

We asked users to respond to these questions after they completed the tasks:

  1. What frustrated you most about this application?
  2. What did you like about this application?
  3. If you had a magic wand, which part of this app would you change?
  4. After using this application, do you feel any different toward your current cycle tracking methods?”

User Testing Results and Analysis

  • All 3 users reported Task 1 as very easy.
  • 2 users reported Task 2 as very easy, 1 as quite difficult; Accordingly, 2 out of 3 users were sure that they completed the task correctly and 1 reported that they encountered an error or a big.
  • All users reported Task 3 as very easy; All users were sure that they completed the task correctly.

Overall, users did very well on the tasks. They completed tasks at their own speed, with limited hang ups. Almost all tasks were marked as “very easy.” Only one task was marked by one user as a 2 out of 5 on the rating scale [1=very difficult, 5=very easy], which was caused by confusion about Lorem Ipsum text. One user was confused when they wanted to navigate back to the quiz results page but was unable to. Sadly, we did not include a place to return to results in our prototype without redoing the quiz. One user reported unhappiness with the scheme because they felt that pink is a stereotypical, “outdated way of representing females.” While researching the start-up by looking at their social media accounts, the first iteration of our app was inspired by their brand centered on the color green; we decided to change it to pink to make it our own without fully considering its implications, as this user pointed out. In another iteration, we would consider different colors.

When asked whether using this application made them think differently about their current methods of cycle tracking, all three of them indicated that using our app made some meaningful impact on them.

These responses were incredibly rewarding to us as the developers. These results are in line with our expectations since we tried to make the app as intuitive as possible by following good design principles we learned in class and listening to our peers' feedback.

The frustrations that users reported of being unable to read placeholder text and complete the quiz themselves (the choices were pre-filled in) tells us that the users found themselves genuinely interested in the subject matter, and wanted to fill out the quiz and read the blog. These were pieces that were unfortunately outside the scope of our project.

In summary, these results illustrated that a first time user would be able to navigate around the app and make use of its features quite easily. Users noted that the app was clear and simple to use. We could improve the app by allowing users to easily return to their quiz results so they don't have to complete the quiz again in order to see recommendations. We would also conduct more research to determine if our color scheme makes some users uncomfortable and change accordingly. In future iterations of user testing, we would make sure that placeholder text is in English.

Conclusion and Learnings

This project allowed me and my teammates to have a complete experience of the iterative design process of a mobile interface. Personally, I learned that in order to get the best out of user testing, prototypes need to be as clear as possible to the user. I also learned that users really appreciate interfaces that are easy to use and navigate more than they appreciate the number of features.
